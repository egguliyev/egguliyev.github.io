Ballistic Deficit

Why shaping time matters in radiation detector electronics

What is Ballistic Deficit?

Ballistic deficit occurs when the shaping time of your electronics is shorter than the charge collection time of your detector.

The result: your electronics measure the signal before all the charge arrives. You see a smaller pulse than you should. Energy resolution degrades.

The Rule: Shaping time must be longer than charge collection time to capture the full signal. But longer shaping time means worse performance at high count rates.

No Ballistic Deficit

  • Shaping time > Collection time
  • Full charge captured
  • Good energy resolution
  • Accurate spectroscopy
  • Trade-off: Lower count rate capability

Severe Ballistic Deficit

  • Shaping time < Collection time
  • Partial charge captured
  • Poor energy resolution
  • Peak shift to lower energy
  • Position-dependent response

Why It Matters for Detector Design

CZT/CdTe Detectors:

Thick detectors (2-5 mm) have long charge collection times (100-500 ns). Holes are slow and often trapped. If your ASIC uses fast shaping for high count rates, you lose signal from deep interactions.

The Trade-off:

Fast Shaping → High Count Rate → More Ballistic Deficit

Slow Shaping → Low Count Rate → Full Signal Collection

Solutions:

  • Use small pixel effect (signal from electrons only)
  • Apply depth-of-interaction correction
  • Optimize detector thickness for your energy range
  • Choose ASIC with appropriate shaping time

Real-World Example: Photon-Counting CT

In PCCT, ASICs use very short shaping times (10-20 ns) to handle high flux (25-48 MCPS per pixel). With 2mm CZT and 100+ ns electron collection time, ballistic deficit is unavoidable.

The small pixel effect saves PCCT: by making pixels much smaller than detector thickness, the weighting potential concentrates near the anode. Most signal comes from the last ~100 um of electron drift, matching the fast shaping time.
